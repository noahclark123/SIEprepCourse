Understanding Securities Regulations

Based on insights from Capital Advantage Tutoring

A clear explanation of the major securities regulations that form the foundation of the securities industry. Understanding these regulations is crucial for success on the SIE exam.

Important regulations to understand:

  • Securities Act of 1933 - Often called the "Truth in Securities" law, this act requires that investors receive financial and other significant information concerning securities being offered for public sale, and prohibits deceit, misrepresentations, and other fraud in the sale of securities.
  • Securities Exchange Act of 1934 - Created the SEC and gives it broad authority over all aspects of the securities industry, including the power to register, regulate, and oversee brokerage firms, transfer agents, and clearing agencies as well as the nation's securities self-regulatory organizations (SROs).
  • Investment Company Act of 1940 - Regulates the organization of companies that engage primarily in investing, reinvesting, and trading in securities, and whose own securities are offered to the investing public.
  • Investment Advisers Act of 1940 - Regulates investment advisers and requires firms or sole practitioners compensated for advising others about securities investments to register with the SEC and conform to regulations designed to protect investors.

Equity Securities Explained

Based on insights from Capital Advantage Tutoring

A comprehensive overview of equity securities, their characteristics, and how they function in the market. This knowledge is essential for the Products and Their Risks section of the SIE exam.

Key concepts about equity securities:

  • Common Stock - Represents ownership in a corporation with voting rights. Common stockholders have a residual claim on assets and may receive dividends, though dividends are not guaranteed.
  • Preferred Stock - A hybrid security with characteristics of both stocks and bonds. Preferred stockholders receive fixed dividends before common stockholders but typically don't have voting rights.
  • Rights and Warrants - Rights allow existing shareholders to purchase additional shares at a discount before a new issue. Warrants are long-term options to buy stock at a specified price.
  • American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) - Represent shares in foreign companies and trade on U.S. exchanges, allowing investors to own foreign stocks without the complications of cross-border transactions.

Bond Fundamentals

Based on insights from Capital Advantage Tutoring

An in-depth look at debt securities, their features, and how they differ from equities. Understanding bonds is critical for the Products and Their Risks section of the SIE exam.

Essential bond concepts:

  • Bond Characteristics - Par value, coupon rate, maturity date, and yield are fundamental characteristics of bonds that determine their value and risk profile.
  • Types of Bonds - Treasury securities (bills, notes, bonds), municipal bonds (general obligation, revenue), corporate bonds (secured, unsecured), and agency bonds each have unique features and risk profiles.
  • Bond Pricing - Bond prices move inversely to interest rates. When interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa.
  • Bond Risks - Interest rate risk, credit risk, inflation risk, liquidity risk, and call risk are the primary risks associated with bond investments.

Test-Taking Strategies for the SIE Exam

Based on insights from Series 7 Guru

Practical advice for approaching the SIE exam questions and managing your time effectively during the test. These strategies can help you maximize your score.

Effective test-taking strategies:

  • Read Questions Carefully - Pay close attention to qualifiers like "EXCEPT," "NOT," "BEST," or "MOST" that can change the meaning of a question.
  • Process of Elimination - If unsure about an answer, eliminate obviously incorrect options to improve your odds of selecting the correct answer.
  • Time Management - With 75 questions in 105 minutes, you have about 1.4 minutes per question. Monitor your pace throughout the exam.
  • Flag and Return - If a question is taking too long, flag it and return later rather than spending excessive time on one question.
  • Answer Every Question - There's no penalty for wrong answers, so make an educated guess if necessary rather than leaving questions unanswered.
